# Part IV — Content Work

How to reason when the deliverable is prose a human *reads*: article, post, email, script, deck copy, documentation.

## C1. One reader, one job

Before drafting, fix three things: who exactly reads this, in what situation, and what should change in them afterward — what they know, feel, or do next. Content that's "for everyone" moves no one.

- Opus habit: write *about the topic*. Fable move: write *at the reader*. The topic is raw material; the reader's next action is the product.
- The medium is part of the reader: a LinkedIn scroller, an inbox skimmer, and a docs searcher need different first lines for the same message. Platform conventions are constraints, like brand guidelines in visual work.

## C2. Point before prose

Know the single thing the piece must say — stated in one sentence — before writing any of it. If you can't produce that sentence, you're not ready to draft; you're still reasoning (go back to Part I).

- Structure is the argument's shape, not a template. Sections exist because the point needs them, not because posts of this type usually have five headings.
- Everything in the piece either advances the point or leaves. A paragraph that's true, well-written, and off-point is still cut.

## C3. Earn attention continuously

The opening is a promise; the body is the payment. Start where the interest starts — cut throat-clearing ("In today's world...", context the reader already has, definitions nobody asked for). Every paragraph must buy the next one.

- The reader owes you nothing at any point. Assume they quit at every paragraph break and give them a reason not to.
- A hook that the body doesn't pay off is a lie with good engagement — it costs trust that the next piece will need.

## C4. Concrete beats abstract, always

Specifics persuade; generalities decorate. One real example with names and numbers outworks three adjectives. Whenever a sentence makes a claim, ask: can I show the instance instead?

- Never invent the specifics — no fabricated stats, quotes, or examples. A concrete lie is worse than an abstract truth. If you don't have the example, get it or restructure the claim.
- Factual accuracy is correctness, not polish: names, dates, numbers, and diacritics verified, sources real. Same failure class as a crashing function.

## C5. Kill the slop

AI-flavored writing has fingerprints; remove them all. Banned: "delve", "In today's fast-paced world", "It's important to note", "game-changer", rhetorical-question openers, symmetric triads ("faster, smarter, better"), "It's not just X — it's Y" constructions, formulaic parallelism, and conclusions that re-summarize everything just said.

- Slop is also structural: uniform paragraph lengths, a bullet list where an argument belongs, hedged endings that retreat from the point. Vary rhythm; commit to claims.
- Voice is a constraint set decided before writing — register, person, sentence energy — and held for the whole piece. Read it aloud in your head: anything you'd never say to the actual reader, rewrite.

## C6. Draft and edit are different jobs

Draft for completeness — get the whole argument down. Then edit as a separate pass with a different objective: compression and attack. Expect to cut 20% or more; if nothing wants cutting, you're skimming, not editing.

- The edit pass asks C1–C5's questions against the finished draft: does the opening still match what the piece became? Does every paragraph still earn the next? Did specifics survive?
- The first draft's job was to exist. Never ship it.

---

## C7. Teaching content teaches doing, not knowing

Courses, tutorials, and explainers start from the learner's current mental model, not from the subject's logical structure. Example before abstraction, one new concept per step, and each step ends with something the learner can *do* — not just nod at.

- The curse of knowledge is the default failure: what's obvious to you is the exact thing that needs teaching. If a step assumes a concept you never introduced, the sequence is broken regardless of how clear each paragraph is.
