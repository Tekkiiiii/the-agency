# Pricing Strategies (7)

Pricing is copy. The number, the order, and the framing change conversion as much as the headline. Pair these with `psychology.md` (Anchoring, Decoy, Loss Aversion, Risk Reversal).

---

## 1. Anchoring

**Move** — show a high reference number first so every later number feels small.

**Patterns**
- Strikethrough MSRP next to today's price.
- "Compare to enterprise [vendor] at $50K/year — ours is $99/mo."
- "Hiring this in-house: $120K/yr. With us: $1,200/mo."

**Best when** — selling against a known-expensive alternative, premium-positioned products.

**Pitfall** — fake anchors (an MSRP that no one ever paid) get caught and demolish trust.

---

## 2. Value Stacking

**Move** — list every component of the offer with its standalone value, then total it before showing the actual price.

**Patterns**
- "Includes: [thing 1] ($X), [thing 2] ($Y), [thing 3] ($Z), [bonus] ($W). Total value: $X+Y+Z+W. Today: $T."
- Course landing pages, info products, bundles.

**Best when** — high-ticket offers, info products, agency packages, B2B retainers.

**Pitfall** — inflated component prices (the "$2,000 worksheet") read as scammy. Components must hold up under solo scrutiny.

---

## 3. Cost of Inaction

**Move** — quantify what staying with the status quo costs over a defined timeframe. Make doing nothing feel expensive.

**Patterns**
- "5 hours/week × 52 weeks × your hourly rate = $X this year you're already paying."
- "Every month you wait, churn compounds at [%]."
- "By the time you 'get around to it,' the problem has cost you [outcome]."

**Best when** — productivity tools, B2B SaaS, finance, insurance, health.

**Pitfall** — inflated savings math that the buyer can't reproduce.

---

## 4. Tiered Pricing with Decoy

**Move** — three tiers where the middle one is engineered to win. The high tier exists to make middle look obvious; the low tier exists to make middle look generous.

**Patterns**
- Basic / **Pro** / Premium → Pro at the visual center, slightly larger card, "Most Popular" badge
- Mini / **Standard** / Family → Family per-unit cheapest but Standard the sweet spot
- Pricing tables on SaaS landing pages

**Best when** — SaaS, subscriptions, consumer subscriptions, course tiers, physical bundles.

**Pitfall** — three plans that all look reasonable means no decoy at all. The high tier should be visibly "too much" for most buyers.

---

## 5. Charm Pricing & Threshold Pricing

**Move** — exploit numerical perception edges.

**Patterns**
- $9.99 vs $10 (charm)
- $19 vs $20 (single-digit drop crosses threshold)
- $4.95/mo vs $59/yr — show monthly first to compress sticker shock
- "$1/day" instead of "$30/mo"

**Best when** — DTC, consumer subscriptions, low-consideration purchases.

**Pitfall** — luxury and B2B suffer from charm pricing — round numbers signal confidence at the high end.

---

## 6. Risk Reversal Pricing

**Move** — make the offer reversible so saying yes carries no downside. Often combined with money-back, free trial, or pay-on-results.

**Patterns**
- "30-day money back, no questions, no forms."
- "Free for 14 days. Cancel anytime in one click."
- "Pay only when [outcome]. If you don't [outcome], it's free."
- "We'll migrate your data for free. Hate it? You keep everything."

**Best when** — high-ticket, new categories, audiences with low trust, finance/insurance.

**Pitfall** — guarantee buried in T&Cs is worth zero. Lead with it; make it visible.

---

## 7. Bundle vs Unbundle (Pricing Funnel)

**Move** — choose between one fat package (Bundle) or modular building blocks (Unbundle) based on buyer mode.

**Bundle** — one all-inclusive price; reduces choice fatigue; signals confidence.
- "Everything you need, one price. $X/mo."
- Best for: novices, time-poor buyers, gift purchases.

**Unbundle** — pay only for what you use; expands TAM; rewards expertise.
- "Pick what you need. Add modules anytime."
- Best for: experts, cost-sensitive buyers, large enterprises.

**Combo move** — offer both: a flagship Bundle + à la carte Unbundle for power users.

**Pitfall** — defaulting to Unbundle when the audience is novice (they bounce on choice). Defaulting to Bundle when the audience is enterprise (they want line items).
